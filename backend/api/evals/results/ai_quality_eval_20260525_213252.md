Prompt: Ok what is IV crush

Provider: `gemini` / `gemini-2.5-flash` / fallback=`False`

Mode: `concept`

Answer:

IV crush happens when the market's expectation of a stock's future price movement, known as Implied Volatility (IV), drops sharply. This usually occurs right after a major event like an earnings report or a big news announcement.

Before the event, IV is high because there's a lot of uncertainty about how the stock will react. Once the event passes, that uncertainty is resolved, and IV quickly "crushes" or falls back down.

For options traders, this means that even if the stock moves in your favor, the sudden drop in IV can significantly reduce the value of your options, making it harder to profit or even leading to a loss. It's a key risk for options bought just before known events.

### PASS - compare_call_debit_spread

Prompt: Compare a long call and a call debit spread for earnings

Provider: `fallback` / `deterministic-options-coach` / fallback=`True`

Mode: `strategy_explainer`

Answer:

For an earnings trade, a long call is the cleaner upside bet: one contract, max loss is the premium, and upside is open-ended. The problem is that it pays full price for time and implied volatility, so IV crush can hurt even if direction is right.

A call debit spread buys one call and sells a higher-strike call. Max loss is still the net debit paid, but max gain is capped. The trade-off is lower cost and lower breakeven pressure versus giving up the huge upside tail.
